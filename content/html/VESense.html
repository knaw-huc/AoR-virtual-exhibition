Peter Abelard/ Petrus Abaelardus (c. 1079-1142)

Peter Abelard was a scholastic philosopher. He studied with Roscelin and later with William of Champeaux in Paris, at the cathedral school of Notre Dame. Abelard writes in his autobiographal letter The Story of My Troubles (Historia calamitatum) that he fell out with his master, because he defeated William in argument. Abelard set up a school of his own in Corbeil, near Paris. A third period of study followed, when Abelard decided to study theology with Anselmus of Laon. From the mid-1130’s he taught in the schools on the Mont Ste.-Genevieve in Paris. Among Abelard’s students were three popes, several heads of state and, notably, Heloise d’Argentueil, with whom he corresponded until the end of his life (see women and disputation ±a±4wd±a±)

Abelard is called “the greatest logician of the middle ages”[1] Among his early works on logic is the Dialectica. It was written before 1117 and based on lectures he gave in Paris in 1115-6. The book is addressed to his brother Dagobert. The only manuscript witness is Paris, BnF, lat. 14614

Abelard’s logical writings stand in the tradition of the logica vetus He wrote comments on Aristotle’s On Interpretation (Glossae super Periermeneias Aristotelis), on Aristotle’s Categories (Glossae super Praedicamenta Aristotelis and on Porphyry’s Isagoge (Glossae super Porphyrium) – together they formed the Logica ‘ingredientibus’ (Logic for Beginners)

±col2±

Among Abelard’s most famous works are his Dialogue of a Philosopher with a Christian and a Jew and his Sic et non (Yes and No or For and Against), in which he collected opinions on disputed matters, taken from Scripture and the church fathers, which appeared to contradict one another. Sic et non is regarded as a landmark in the history of critical thinking and an important step in development of the scholastic method of the disputatio.

Yet here too Abelard builds on a tradition. In the margins of early medieval manuscripts, centuries before Abelard, annotators weighed and assessed statements of authorities, so they could be used to construct a logical and persuasive argument. Unfortunately there is not a single digitized medieval manuscript of Abelard’s Sic et non to show in this exhibition. We can, however, show a sixteenth-century paper transcript: Cambridge, Corpus Christi College, 165.

On a flyleaf a note taken from the work of Trithemius (1462-1516), with his assessment of ‘Peter, the dialectician of Paris’ Trithemius refers to the council of Sens of 1141 where the ‘depraved doctrine’ of Abelard was condemned, thanks to ‘the agency of Saint Bernard’. It was, however, not for writing the Sic et non that Abelard was condemned but for his theological writings.

According to his contemporaries, Abelard was unbeatable in debate. Supporters and opponents testify that he never lost an argument. When his theological writings attracted the attention of abbot Bernard of Clairvaux, who objected to Abelard’s philosophical analysis of the Trinity and his rational approach to matters of faith, Abelard asked the archbishop of Sense to arrange a public disputation between himself and Bernard.

±col2span±

[[[Bernardus.jpg]]]

The disputation never took place. Bernard appointed a committee to review Abelard’s work on suspicion of heresy. The council of Sens in 1141 condemned nineteen propositions as heretical. Abelard was sentenced to silence.

±col2±

Already twenty years earlier, at the council of Soissons in 1121, Abelard’s writings had been examined and condemned. His book On the Divine Unity and Trinity was burned, and Abelard was required to make a public statement of faith, like Gottschalk of Orbais, Felix of Urgel and Nestorius of Alexandria before him.

±col3±

After the condemnation at Sens in 1141, when he was condemned to silence, Abelard wrote two statements of faith, one private, which he addressed to Heloise (Confessio fidei ad Heloisam) and one public: Confessio fidei ‘universis’: a statement of faith ‘to all’
