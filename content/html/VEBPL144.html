In this manuscript, wide margins are provided to allow ample commentary: a space for them has been marked off by vertical lines on either side of the text. The margin takes up as much as 70% of the page. A first, contemporary set of commentary texts have been copied in the prepared space around the text, but in addition a second, later layer (last quarter of the 13th century) has interacted with the text in a very lively and personal way, adding notes, drawings and peculiar attention signs.

Boethius wrote his Consolation around the year 523. The book is a dialogue between Lady Philosophy and himself. Philosophy consoles him in the hard times he had fallen on: accused of treason, he was incarcerated and in 524 executed by the King of the Ostrogoths Theoderic the Great. In the book, Philosophy emphasizes the transient meaning of earthly fortune, such as fame and wealth, and argues for the superiority of things of the mind: happiness comes from within, from wisdom and understanding.

±col2±

The book engages with Christian themes such as free will and predestination, but approaches these from the perspective of natural philosophy and Greek classical philosophy alone. It is an example in how to apply ancient philosophical ideas to subjects that are very Christian in nature.

The book was very popular in the Western literary tradition. Many copies survive, together with translations, commentaries and adaptations.

On this opening, we see some prliminary material on the left (f. 1v), and the beginning of Boethius’ Consolation of Philosophy (f. 2r) on the right [link to text portrait]. This work, which was written part in poetry (opening lines on this page), part in prose (last six lines on this page) was very popular in medieval schools. It had beautiful poetry and prose ánd appropriate moral lessons: it could thus be used for the teaching of Latin grammar and style, ánd for moral education and philosophy. That the text was used for education, is clear from the lay-out of the page: it is such that a large marginal space is provided for commentary (70% of the page!), and that notes could be added between the lines for a more detailed explanation of words, their grammatical form and their meaning.

By looking closely at this page from the Martianus Capella part of the manuscript (f. 66v), one can see how a full grid of ruled lines was provided to write the dense lines of commentary. In the ruling pattern, one line of text equalled two lines of commentary. The ruling lines were created with a sharp point: a pattern of indented/ridged lines is the result.

Zooming in on this first page of the text, we see that not only is the margin extra wide, but also the space in between the lines, to house glosses. Interlinear glosses and marginal notes have been added to the text in a hand that matches the hand of the text. These can be dated to the last quarter of the 12th century. A second set of notes has also been added to the text. The type of script used in these suggests that they were written in the last quarter of the thirteenth century.

The second annotator shows his personality through his annotations: he uses faces, pointing hands, and fanciful creatures to draw attention to particular places on the page. He also adds little sketches such as here on f. 10r the ‘Wheel of Fortune’: it illustrates the famous description of Boethius of the inevitable cycle of human fame and downfall, from the top to the bottom and up again (Boethius, Consolation of Philosophy, Book 2, Chapter 1).

Note that the two hands even have different ways of calling attention to certain elements in the text: they both use ‘Nota’ to signal important elements in the text, but in the original layer, the ‘Nota’ is written in a different way, and sometimes not written at all, but replaced with a head, a hand or even a foot (?)! . On this page (f. 18r), two ‘talking heads’ are drawn, one of them wearing a ‘cappa’ or hat, the other not. Graduating from the medieval university involved a dress code: the new master was to wear a cappa, a certain cloak and certain shoes. So here, we could suggest that this is a drawing of a master and a student talking: the process of explaining the text, of understanding it by reading it together, is illustrated.

It is clear that the pages of this manuscript have been slightly cut along the edges of the pages. Some text and some drawings are now incomplete. The cutting of pages was indeed common practice when a book was rebound. Yet even before the cutting, the placement of the faces, hands and other pointers was probably deliberately close to the edge of the page: when browsing through the manuscript, the marked passages would pop out immediately.

In the part of the book with Martianus Capella’s De nuptiis, the face of the page changes. Here (f. 57v-58r) there was a large, existing commentary that was copied in the margin. There was hardly any space, so it seems, for other, more spontanuous material in the margins. The active and engaged look and feel of the annotated pages in the previous part shifts to a more passive look and feel.

Boethius’ Consolation of Philosophy and Martianus’ De nuptiis do not have much in common concerning their contents, but their form is similar: they mix poetry and prose. That this was an important point of attration for the readers is testified by many notes concerning the analysis of the poetic metres used by the authors. Here, on an added page (f. 82v-83r, detail), a complete scheme of poetic metres has been drawn.

On occasion, later hands added other layers of notes to the text. Here (f. 85v-86r), for example, a hand added an extensive note in a very faded cursive hand, hardly legible anymore. Perhaps it is a draft of a new text, added in a space that was still empty. The writer used a dry pen or lead pencil.

The material state of this manuscript is not great. The text is executed with style and precision, but it was not written on top quality parchment. It was a functional book made for use and study, not for display.

At the very end of the book (f. 93v-94r), a single page has been added (upside down) from a different mansucript: it is a page dated to around 1100 with a fragment from Macrobius’commentary on Cicero’s Dream of Scipio. The page is a loose leaf. Although the combination could be entirely random, the theme of the two texts are similar: in Martianus the way in which the universe works, how the planets revolve in their paths around the earth, is explained, and in Macrobius too. That this was a point of interest for the 13th-century annotator is testified by tiny figures visualizing the different models, added to the text of Martianus (f. 90r).
