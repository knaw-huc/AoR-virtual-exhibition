This early tenth-century manuscript of logical works displays an abundance of glosses. It is a veritable treasure trove that tells us much about the reception of logic and rational argumentation. The different users of this manuscript put much effort in creating a rich dossier of knowledge pertaining to the art of reasoning, and drew in snippets of useful knowledge from a variety of sources and traditions.[1]

±col2±

The manuscript was copied in the early tenth century and belonged to the library of St-Germain in Auxerre, where it had the shelf mark N. 1108 (olim 442). The codex was restored in 1977.

It is a composite manuscript consisting of six quires. As can be seen clearly in this photo, offering a view of the tail of the codex, the quires have various dimensions. On the left, quires 1 to 4, containing Aristotle’s Periermeneias and the densely annotated Categoriae decem (dimensions: 250-255 x 200), on the right, quires 5 and 6 (dimensions 270 x 220). Quire 6 contains Boethius’ Opuscula sacra. Inserts are attached to the spine to compensate for the difference in size.

[[[12949tail.jpg]]]

±row±

±col1±

The glosses in this manuscript go back to, or can be associated with different masters: John Scot Eriugena, Heiric of Auxerre, Israel Scotus (aka Israel the Grammarian) and a certain Aldelm who was probably also an Irish scholar [Contreni, 1977]. The manuscript is richly annotated by several hands. Some notes are contemporary to the hand of the main text, others are slightly later. Throughout the codex slips of parchment have been inserted. A remarkable feature of the layout of the glosses in this manuscript is that the notes are not only written horizontally but also vertically, across the length of the page - a very economic use of space

The first page, with Aristotle’s On Interpretation (Peri Hermeneias) invited quite a number of annotations, but they peter out on the next pages. Aristotle’s On Interpretation deals with the relationship between language and reality. This work would receive much more attention in the twelfth century.

On folio 12 recto we find Augustine’s De dialectica, a brief exposition on the ambiguity of language and the etymology of words. Augustine said he never finished the De dialectica and lost his only copy of the manuscript, yet his authorship of this text is no longer in doubt. Above is an introduction (accessus) to the author, Augustine, and an etymology of the word dialectica, with a reference to John Scot Eriugenia [LINK].

±row±

±col1±

Below, marked in orange, start De dialectica proper, with the well-known opening words: “Dialectia est bene disputandi scientia”; “Dialectic is the art of arguing well.”

±col2span±

[[[12949f12rfr1.jpg]]]

±row±

±col1±

Wedged between the short introduction to Augustine and the text of De dialectica itself, we encounter the saying attributed to the philosopher Zeno [LINK]: “Rhetoric is an open hand, dialectic a clenched fist.”[2] It is part of a longer quotation, taken from Alcuin’s De dialectica, in which Alcuin stated that devoted teachers of dialectic are rare, and that only few are able to fully understand it and investigate it properly.

On folio 23 verso, in the margin of a fragment of an unidentified text that discusses ousia, esssence, a memory aid has been added to help the reader remember the distinction between catascheva (confirmation) and anascheva (refutation):

Catascheva confirmatio (Catascheva confirmation)
Anascheva refutation (Anascheva refutation)
Pudet me vanitatis (I am ashamed of my empty pride)
erubesco per vanitatem (I blush because of empty pride)
Pudet me fratris (I am ashamed of my brother)
Pudet me magistri (I am ashamed of my master)

On folio 24r is the opening of the Categoriae decem (Ten Categories), also known as the Paraphrasis Themistiana. The title reads: “The Categories of Aristotle translated by Augustine from Greek into Latin.”[3] This text was ascribed to Augustine in the early middle ages. From the eighth to eleventh century the Categoriae decem drew by far the most attention of scholars and students interested in logic. This interest is also evident from the many annotations the text received this early tenth-century manuscript.

±col2span±

[[[12949f24rfr2.jpg]]]

±row±

±col1±

According to an annotation on f. 25v the glosses (or one strand of them) go back to Heiric of Auxerre (d. 876) [LINK], a student of Lupus of Ferrières and teacher of Remigius and Hucbald. The annotation reads: “Heiricus magister Remigii fecit has glossas” – “Heiric the master of Remigius made these glosses.”

±col2span±

[[[12949f25vfr.jpg]]]

±row±

±col1±

The glosses to the Categoriae decem in this manuscript are partly standard glosses that can be found in many manuscripts of this text, but there is also a strand of glosses (perhaps the ones that are here attributed to Heiric?) that go back to ideas expressed by John Scot Eriugena, in particular in his Periphyseon. John Marenbon, who did not think much of the intellectual rigour of these annotations characterized them as follows: “Many of the Eriugenian glosses seem to be the product of someone whose mind is well stocked with motifs and phrases from the Periphyseon who has set about the task of commenting the Ten Categories after a pint too many.”[4]

On a slip of parchment inserted between folio 23 verso and folio 24recto we find verses of John Scot Eriugena, Bede, Prudentius, Virgil and Persius and an epitaph of Augustine, alongside various anonymous verses and statements. The quotations bear no direct relationship to any of the texts in this codex, apart from the reoccurrence of familiar names. Perhaps the notes were made by a student, scholar or master for personal use and recollection on a scrap of parchment that travelled with this manuscript and was at some point inserted into the codex. Underlined are: versuS iohanni Scotti, bedae, Virgilii, Prudentii, Persii, epitaphium sancti augustini. “Passerby, do you wish to know how poets still live after death? Those words you read, behold I speak. Your voice is very much mine.”[5]

The glosses to Porphyry’s Isagoge, starting on folio 46 recto, consist mainly of excerpts from Boethius’s commentaries, Isidore of Seville, Alcuin’s De dialectica and Macrobius Commentary on the Dream of Scipio. These glosses are attributed to a certain ICPA.

±col2span±

{{> folios/folio-bnf129_46_r }}

±row±

±col1±

f. 52v “I, Icpa, wrote this little book, glossing it in some way - Icpa [rasura] hunc scripsi glossans utcunque labellum.” Édouard Jeanneau has proposed that ‘Icpa’ is the Latin transliteration of the Greek capital letters ΙΞΡΑ (ISRA) and refers to Israel the Grammarian (c. 895 – c. 965). Yet, as this manuscripts dates to the early tenth century, Israel would have been rather young when he copied and glossed Porphyry’s Isagoge.

±col2span±

[[[12949f52vfr.jpg]]]

±row±

On folio 27verso we encounter a note of criticsm attached to a gloss. Written above a discussion in a gloss of ‘man’ being both ‘genus’ and ‘species’, with ‘genus’ referring to man in general and ‘species’ (according to Martianus, the gloss says), to particular people, such as Ethiopians, Africans and Romans, a note in darker ink states: ‘Non est haec glossa a nobis recipienda’ - ‘We should not accept this gloss’

Throughout the codex a number of diagrams are found in a variety of shapes: trees, houses, fishbones. They illustrate the habit of medieval scholars to visualize the knowledge they encountered in the texts.
