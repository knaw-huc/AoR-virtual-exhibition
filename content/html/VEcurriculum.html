What was taught in medieval schools? The Curriculum

In the Carolingian period (ca 780-900), known for its efforts to revive and stimulate education, a teaching programme was promoted which included first and foremost christian learning: knowledge of the bible and the liturgy and the works of the church fathers.

±col2±

With that, however, a whole heritage of prechristian and late-antique learning was also adopted into the curriculum: the Roman poets for their beautiful and complex Latin, the Greek philosophers (in Latin translation) for their systems of understanding the world. The ancient division of learning into seven key disciplines, the seven liberal arts, was also embraced by the medieval world: these became a core element.

The seven liberal arts
In the late-ancient and early medieval knowledge tradition, the body of knowledge (or wisdom, sapientia) was divided up into fields (artes or disciplinae) to make the whole more managable. For the Latin middle ages, the division into seven liberal arts, as found in the works of Martianus Capella and Boethius, was most influential. These seven liberal arts were in turn divided into the three arts of language (trivium) and the four arts of number (quadrivium).

The arts of language: Trivium
Three disciplines formed the art of language: Grammar, Rhetoric and Dialectic.
Grammar covered the basics: young students were introduced into letters, reading and writing, in Latin. One of the basic handbooks used for a first introduction was Donatus, Ars minor. More advanced handbooks were also studied on a higher level: Donatus’ Ars maior and Priscian’s Institutiones grammaticae.
