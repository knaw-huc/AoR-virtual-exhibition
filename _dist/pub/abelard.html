Abelard and the council of Sense

Peter Abelard/ Petrus Abaelardus (c. 1079-1142)

Peter Abelard was a scholastic philosopher. He studied with Roscelin and later with William of Champeaux in Paris, at the cathedral school of Notre Dame. Abelard writes in his autobiographal letter The Story of My Troubles (Historia calamitatum) that he fell out with his master, because he defeated William in argument. Abelard set up a school of his own in Corbeil, near Paris. A third period of study followed, when Abelard decided to study theology with Anselmus of Laon. From the mid-1130’s he taught in the schools on the Mont Ste.-Genevieve in Paris. Among Abelard’s students were three popes, several heads of state and, notably, Heloise d’Argentueil, with whom he corresponded until the end of his life (see women and disputation women and disputation)

Abelard is called “the greatest logician of the middle ages”1 Among his early works on logic is the Dialectica. It was written before 1117 and based on lectures he gave in Paris in 1115-6. The book is addressed to his brother Dagobert. The only manuscript witness is Paris, BnF, lat. 14614

Abelard’s logical writings stand in the tradition of the logica vetus He wrote comments on Aristotle’s On Interpretation (Glossae super Periermeneias Aristotelis), on Aristotle’s Categories (Glossae super Praedicamenta Aristotelis and on Porphyry’s Isagoge (Glossae super Porphyrium) – together they formed the Logica ‘ingredientibus’ (Logic for Beginners)

Among Abelard’s most famous works are his Dialogue of a Philosopher with a Christian and a Jew and his Sic et non (Yes and No or For and Against), in which he collected opinions on disputed matters, taken from Scripture and the church fathers, which appeared to contradict one another. Sic et non is regarded as a landmark in the history of critical thinking and an important step in development of the scholastic method of the disputatio.

Yet here too Abelard builds on a tradition. In the margins of early medieval manuscripts, centuries before Abelard, annotators weighed and assessed statements of authorities, so they could be used to construct a logical and persuasive argument. Unfortunately there is not a single digitized medieval manuscript of Abelard’s Sic et non to show in this exhibition. We can, however, show a sixteenth-century paper transcript: Cambridge, Corpus Christi College, 165.

On a flyleaf a note taken from the work of Trithemius (1462-1516), with his assessment of ‘Peter, the dialectician of Paris’ Trithemius refers to the council of Sens of 1141 where the ‘depraved doctrine’ of Abelard was condemned, thanks to ‘the agency of Saint Bernard’. It was, however, not for writing the Sic et non that Abelard was condemned but for his theological writings.

According to his contemporaries, Abelard was unbeatable in debate. Supporters and opponents testify that he never lost an argument. When his theological writings attracted the attention of abbot Bernard of Clairvaux, who objected to Abelard’s philosophical analysis of the Trinity and his rational approach to matters of faith, Abelard asked the archbishop of Sense to arrange a public disputation between himself and Bernard.

[[[Bernardus.jpg]]]

The disputation never took place. Bernard appointed a committee to review Abelard’s work on suspicion of heresy. The council of Sens in 1141 condemned nineteen propositions as heretical. Abelard was sentenced to silence.

Already twenty years earlier, at the council of Soissons in 1121, Abelard’s writings had been examined and condemned. His book On the Divine Unity and Trinity was burned, and Abelard was required to make a public statement of faith, like Gottschalk of Orbais, Felix of Urgel and Nestorius of Alexandria before him.

After the condemnation at Sens in 1141, when he was condemned to silence, Abelard wrote two statements of faith, one private, which he addressed to Heloise (Confessio fidei ad Heloisam) and one public: Confessio fidei ‘universis’: a statement of faith ‘to all’

In the margin of Bn Flat. 2923, f.49r accusations of heresy (all starting with contra….) against which Abelard defends himself. A textual disputation to make up for the public disputation that was denied to him? The manuscript belonged to Petrarch after 1337. Abelard’s private statement of faith addressed to Heloise, Confessio ad Heloisam, has been preserved in the Apologia of Abelard’s student Berengar of Potiers.

Porphyry’s Isagoge

Porphyry was a philosopher from Tyre, Phoenicia (today Lebanon), active in the third century C.E. He wrote on all the relevant branches of knowledge of the time, including philosophy, religion, grammar, and natural science, but only a portion of his work survived.

 

He became an important exponent of Neoplatonism. the dominant philosophical current of the period that reinterpreted the ideas of the ancient Greek philosopher Plato. In his philosophical writings Porphyry attempted to conciliate Aristotle’s logical works with the Platonic doctrine. 

Porphyry is best known for the Isagoge, an introduction to Aristotle's logical works that soon became an integral part of the Organon (the set of logical works of Aristotle) and one of the central texts for the study of logic in the medieval curriculum.

 

The Isagoge is an elucidation to Aristotle’s doctrine on the five universals or predicables: genus, species, difference, property, and accident. Each of these explain what a substance consists of. Taking a human for example, genus is “animal”, species “man”, difference “rational”, property “able to speak”, accident “white”. Porphyry’s Isagoge, in the Latin translation by Boethius, became widely influential in the Latin West and one of the basic introductory texts for the study of philosophy for at least 1000 years.

His contemporaries, and especially his students, appreciated Abelard for his commentaries and glosses on the curriculum of the logica vetus. He livened up his lectures with self-ironic examples to amuse his students and make dry logic more palpable, such as “His girl loves Peter” as the converse of “Peter loves his girl”. In the glosses that have survived in Munich, Clm 14479 he made fun of his own slight stature when illustrating different types of contradiction: … it can apply equally to both “Peter will close this door, Peter will not close this door”; or it relates more to another, as “Peter will fall into the latrine, Peter will not fall into the latrine” relates more to falling, because he is small, even if great in forbearance. Munich, Clm 14479, f. 53v , translation Mews, 2004, 35

Among modern scholars, Abelard is less known for his glosses on logic than for a work that he wrote around 1120, Sic et non. In Sic et non (For and Against) he placed 158 opposing arguments and seemingly conflicting statements from scriptural and patristic authorities against one another, to stimulate reflection and debate. In the preface he formulates the purpose of his collection: “by doubting we come to question, and by questioning we arrive at truth”. Sic et non is commonly regarded as a landmark in the history of critical thinking and an important step in development of the scholastic method of the disputatio. [Novikoff, Weijers, Rexroth]

Abelard’s Sic et non is, however, not as innovative as would appear at first sight. The method of collecting apparently contradicting statements was widely applied among the glossators of Roman law at the end of the eleventh century [Flechner], and traces of a similar method of weighing and assessing authorities can be found in the margins and blank spaces of early medieval manuscripts

Here we see a sixteenth-century paper transcript of Abelard’s Sic et non. On a flyleaf a note is added, taken from the work of abbot Trithemius of Trondheim (1462-1516), who wrote an assessment of ‘Peter, the dialectician of Paris’ (Petrus dialecticus Parisiensis) In the note, Trithemius refers to the council of Sens of 1141 where the ‘depraved doctrine’ of Abelard was condemned, thanks to ‘the efforts of Saint Bernard’.

It was, however, not for writing the Sic et non that Abelard was condemned but for his theological treatises. His philosophical analysis of the Trinity caused a stir among ecclesiastical authorities such as Bernardus of Clairvaux, who charged him with heresy. Abelard’s theological writings, the ensuing condemnations, and his illicit love affair with Heloise d’Argenteuil, are discussed in another section of this exhibition Abelard and the council of Sense.

Abelard’s logical works sprang from lectures he gave to his students and occasionally preserve classroom debates [Novikoff 2014, 331]. He kept revising his work as he changed his view. As a result of this working method, which was in fact very similar to that of Aristotle himself (Kennedy, 1999, 75), his work has survived in different versions in often incomplete manuscripts. This is why most of his writings are hard to date and provide Abelard-scholars with a headache. At the same time, the complicated circumstances of transmission make studying Abelard all the more interesting, because the different versions, gaps, revisions, additions and corrections allow the reader to see a scholar’s mind at work.
