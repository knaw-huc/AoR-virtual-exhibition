Paris, BnF, lat. 12949

A rich dossier on the art of reasoning1

Paris, BnF, lat. 12949 is a composite manuscript consisting of six quires of varying dimensions, as can be clearly seen in the image on the right. The manuscript was restored in 1977. The glosses in this manuscript go back to, or can be associated with different masters: John Scottus and his assistant, Heiric, Remigius and the School of Auxerre,, Israel Scotus (aka Israel the Grammarian) and a certain Aldelm who was probably also an Irish scholar [Contreni, 1977]

Paris, BnF, lat. 12949 contains a number of logical works and three of Boethius’ theological treatises known as the Opuscula sacra. It was copied in the early tenth century and belonged to the library of St-Germain in Auxerre, where it had the shelf mark N. 1108 (olim 442).

[[[olim442.jpg]]]

The manuscript is richly annotated by several hands. Some notes are contemporary to the hand of the main text, others are slightly later. Throughout the codex slips of parchment have been inserted (12 in total), providing extra comments to the text. Perhaps they were added because every inch of the page was already filled with notes and more space was needed, but there is also another explanation (see below). A remarkable feature of the layout of the glosses in this manuscript is that the notes are not only written horizontally but also vertically, across the length of the page - a very economic use of space.

Although the first page invited quite a number of annotations, they peter out on the next pages. Aristotle’s On Interpretation deals with the relationship between language and reality. This work would receive much more attention in the twelfth century (see for example. Abelard’s Glosses super Periermeneias)

Fol. 12r shows the opening of Augustine’s De dialectica, a brief exposition on the ambiguity and etymology of words. Augustine writes in his Retractationes that he never finished the book and he lost his only copy of the manuscript. Nevertheless his authorship of the text is (no longer) in doubt.

Wedged between the short introduction to Augustine and the text of De dialectica itself, we encounter the saying attributed to the philosopher Zeno: “Rhetoric is an open hand, dialectic a clenched fist.” (Nota. Dialectica nempe est quidam pugnus astrictus, sicut et rhetorica palma quaedam extensa) It is part of a longer quotation, taken from Alcuin’s De dialectica, in which he states that devoted teachers of dialectic are rare. Only few are able to fully understand it and investigate it properly.

f. 12r Opening of the Categoriae decem (Ten Categories). Also known as the Paraphrasis Themistiana. The text dates to the fourth century. In the early middle ages it was ascribed to Augustine and it was taken to be a translation of Aristotle’s On Interpretation instead of a paraphrase

On f. 23v, in the margin of a fragment with a discussion of ousia, esssence, a memory aid has been added to help remember what catascheva means and what again anascheva:

Catascheva est firmatio (Catascheva is a confirmation)
Anascheva refutation (Anascheva a refutation)
Pudet me vanitatis (I am ashamed of my empty pride)
erubesco per vanitatem (I blush because of empty pride)
Pudet me fratris (I am ashamed of my brothers)
Pudet me magistri (I am ashamed of my master)

On f. 24r we see the opening of the Categoriae decem (Ten Categories), also known as the Paraphrasis Themistiana. This Latin paraphrase of Aristotle’s Categories (Praedicamenta or Categoriae) dates to the fourth century. In the early middle ages it was ascribed to Augustine, and taken to be a translation of Aristotle’s work rather than a paraphrase. From the eighth to eleventh the Categoriae decem drew by far the most attention of scholars and students interested in logic. This interest is also evident from the many annotations the text received this early tenth-century manuscript.

Above the opening of the text is the verse preface that Alcuin added to the Categoriae decem, addressed to King Charlemagne. Alcuin was responsible for the re-circulationof the Ten Categories in the late eighth century, when interest in logic and especailly in the relation between language, logic and ontology increased in the aftermath of the debate with Byzantium on the veneration of images. Alcuin may have been the first to ascribe the Ten Categories to Augustine. His dedicatory verses to Charlemagne have been added to the Categoriae decem in the majority of manuscripts that transmit this work, including the oldest manuscript, Vatican, BAV, Pagès 1, dated between 780 and 813. This manuscript was owned by bishop Leidrad of Lyon who added corrections. Go to Vatican, BAV, Pagès 1, screen 14, to see Leidrad’s autograph owner’s mark https://digi.vatlib.it/view/MSS_Pages.1

According to an annotation on f. 25v the glosses (or one strand of them) go back to Heiric of Auxerre (see also, Heiric, Remigius and the School of Auxerre) (d. 876), a student of Lupus of Ferrières and teacher of Remigius and Hucbald. The annotation reads: “Heiricus magister Remigii fecit has glossas” – “Heiric the master of Remigius made these glosses”

The glosses to the Categoriae decem in this manuscript are partly standard glosses that can be found in many manuscripts of this tekst, but there is also a strand of glosses (perhaps the ones that are here attributed to Heiric?) that go back to ideas expressed by John Scot Eriugena, in particular in his Periphyseon or De divisione naturae (On the Division of Nature) The work was condemned in 1225 at the council at Sens and ended up on the Roman Index of Forbidden Books. In the ninth and tenth centuries, however, the Periphyseon was considered the pinnacle of learning. John Marenbon, who did not think much of the intellectual rigour of these annotations characterized them as follows:

“Many of the Eriugenian glosses seem to be the product of someone whose mind is well stocked with motifs and phrases from the Periphyseon who has set about the task of commenting the Ten Categories after a pint too many.”

Paris, BnF, lat. 12949, with its abundance of glosses and added information on inserted leaves, is a veritable treasure trove that tells us much about the reception of logic and rational argumentation. The different users of this manuscript put much effort in creating a rich dossier of knowledge pertaining to the art of reasoning, and drew in snippets of useful knowledge from a variety of sources and traditions.

On a slip of parchment inserted between f. 23v and f. 24r, between the fragment that discusses ousia and the Categoriae decem we find verses of John Scot Eriugena, Bede, Prudentius, Virgil and Persius and an epitaph of Augustine, alongside various anonymous verses and statements. The verses and statements assembled here have no direct relationship to any of the texts in this codex, apart from the reoccurrence of familiar names. Perhaps the notes were made by a student, scholar or master for personal use and recollection on a scrap of parchment that travelled with this manuscript and was at some point inserted into the codex.

The glosses consist mainly of excerpts from Boethius’s commentaries, Isidore of Seville, Alcuin’s De dialectica and Macrobius Commentary on the Dream of Scipio. These glosses are attributed to a certain ICPA.

f.52v: “I, Icpa, wrote this little book, glossing it in some way - Icpa [rasura] hunc scripsi glossas utcunque libellum” Édouard Jeanneau has proposed that ‘Icpa’ is the Latin transliteration of the Greek kapital letters ΙΞΡΑ (ISRA) and refers to Israel the Grammarian (c. 895 – c. 965). Yet, as this manuscripts dates to the early tenth century, Israel would have been rather young when he copy and glossed Porphyry’s Isagoge. An extremely gifted student?

On f. 27v we encounter a note of criticsm attached to a gloss. Written above a discussion in a gloss of ‘man’ being both ‘genus’ and ‘species’, with ‘genus’ referring to man in general and ‘species’ (according to Martianus, the gloss says), to particular people, such as Ethiopians, Africans and Romans, a note in darker ink states: ‘Non est haec glossa a nobis recipienda’ - ‘We should not accept this gloss.’

Boethius’ theological treatises (opuscula sacra) at the end of the codex received far fewer annotations than the other texts in this codex. This opening page of De Trinitate ad Symmachum (f. 53r) has some glosses, but the next folia are virtually empty. At first glance this is somewhat surprising, for it is here that readers could have learned how to apply the theory of logical argumentation in practice. Boethius’ Against Eutychus and Nestorius for example (ff. 62v-70v) demonstrates how categorical logic can be used against heretics. Where are the glosses and notes of the students and scholars who were so eager to fill the margins of the previous quires? Were they not interested in the practical application of logic? That may have been the case, but there is also another explanation for the absence of notes. These quires with Boethius’ Opuscula sacra make up a separate codicological unit that was only later bound together with the other quires. Perhaps they were added to the fully annotated set after the annotators had stopped their active study of the texts.

As can be seen clearly in this photo,offering a view of the tail of the codex, the quires have various dimensions. On the left, quires 1 to 4, containing Aristotle’s Periermeneias and the densely annotated Categoriae decem (dimensions: 250-255 x 200), on the right, quires 5 and 6 (dimensions 270 x 220). Quire 6 contains Boethius’ Opuscula sacra.

Inserts are attached to the spine to compensate for the difference in size.

Throughout the codex a number of diagrams are found in a variety of shapes: trees, houses, fishbones. They illustrate the habit of medieval scholars to visualize the knowledge they encountered in the texts.
