The school of Auxerre

Heiric, Remigius and the School of Auxerre

The monastery of St. Germain at Auxerre was founded in the 5th century, and had an important and influential school in the Carolingian period. Some of its masters were Heiric and Remigius of Auxerre, both known for their copious production of commentaries or gloss-collections.1 Glosses and commentaries

Heiric of Auxerre (841-876)

Heiric was a member of the monastic community at Auxerre from a child’s age on. He himself had had a famous master: Lupus of Ferrières (c. 805-c. 862), a scholar at the court of Charles the Bald. In the chain of teachers, we can place him in an impressive chain.

Alcuin taught (a.o.) Hrabanus Maurus, who taught (o.a.) Lupus of Ferrières, who taught (o.a.) Heiric of Auxerre, who taught (o.a.) Remigius of Auxerre. Heiric of Auxerre was also taught by Haymo of Auxerre, who was taught by Murethach. Remigius of Auxerre was the teacher of Odo of Cluny and others.

Teachers in manuscripts

The art of reasoning, as part of the arts of language in the seven liberal arts curriculum, was taught in schools at monasteries, cathedrals and later also universities. Traces in medieval manuscripts show the practices of education in multiple ways. One of them is the glosses and notes we find in the margin of many manuscripts. They reflect the way in which a text was used by the teacher in the classroom.

Generally speaking, these glosses and notes are anonymous: they are nameless voices who add their explanations, associations, and synonyms or other thoughts to the text. But in rare cases, we find the names of some masters.

In many manuscripts we find glosses and notes in the margin. They reflect the way in which a text was used by the teacher in the classroom. In Paris, BnF, Lat. 12949, glosses are ascribed to a certain master: Heiric of Auxerre

Paris, BnF, Lat. 12949 is a manuscript with a set of texts on logic. On this opening page, f. 1r, the Latin translation of Aristotle’s On Interpretation start: INCIPIUNT PERIERMENIAE ARISTOTELIS. In the margins and in between the lines, many notes have been added, even across the page.

Here, on f. 24r, we see a text which is even more heavily glossed: the whole space around the text is filled with comments. It is the Latin translation of Aristotle’s Categories.

Here on f. 25v a title has been added to the notes: ‘Heiricus magister Remigii fecit has glossas’, ‘Heiric, the master of Remigius, made these glosses’. It is a rare piece of evidence: authors of glosses are seldom identified.

Remigius of Auxerre: champion of commentaries?

Remigius, the last of the great teachers of Auxerre, has been credited with the composition of around 20 commentaries! It is a matter of debate whether he actually authored all these commentaries, compiled them from existing commentary traditions, or had them compiled by students and scholars in his school.

Porphyry’s Isagoge

Porphyry was a philosopher from Tyre, Phoenicia (today Lebanon), active in the third century C.E. He wrote on all the relevant branches of knowledge of the time, including philosophy, religion, grammar, and natural science, but only a portion of his work survived.

 

He became an important exponent of Neoplatonism. the dominant philosophical current of the period that reinterpreted the ideas of the ancient Greek philosopher Plato. In his philosophical writings Porphyry attempted to conciliate Aristotle’s logical works with the Platonic doctrine. 

Porphyry is best known for the Isagoge, an introduction to Aristotle's logical works that soon became an integral part of the Organon (the set of logical works of Aristotle) and one of the central texts for the study of logic in the medieval curriculum.

 

The Isagoge is an elucidation to Aristotle’s doctrine on the five universals or predicables: genus, species, difference, property, and accident. Each of these explain what a substance consists of. Taking a human for example, genus is “animal”, species “man”, difference “rational”, property “able to speak”, accident “white”. Porphyry’s Isagoge, in the Latin translation by Boethius, became widely influential in the Latin West and one of the basic introductory texts for the study of philosophy for at least 1000 years.
