Codex MS 315(605)

Binding:

Einsiedeln, Stiftsbibliothek Cod. 315 (605) is a tenth-century manuscript, now encased in a fourteenth-century tooled alum taw binding. Faint traces of the volume’s title can still be seen on the cover.

Contents:

The manuscript contains a copy of Boethius’ second commentary on the Isagoge of Porphyry. The title of this five-book text, ‘liber Boetii in isagoges porphyrii’,was written on this page by the fourteenth-century librarian of Einsieldeln, Heinrich von Ligerz. He also added the extended pointing finger (manicula) pointing to the title. It is possible that the manuscript was bound in a limp, soft, cover prior to being backed with hard boards in the fourteenth century. Leaving the first page of the manuscript back (and starting the text on its verso) reduced the chance that text would be lost if the outer binding was damaged.

Scribal collaboration:

The manuscript was copied by multiple scribes. Here we can see two pages where the scribe switches half-way through the page. We can imagine that if a text was to be copied at speed from an exemplar, it was literally a case of ‘all hands on deck’, with one scribe picking up where another left off, even in the middle of a line.

Traces of readership:

The manuscript does not have many annotations, but still holds evidence of reader practices.

While the preceding set of corrections and markups suggested that the text was prepared for attentive reading, the marginal activity here suggests otherwise! A half-finished doodle of a bearded face brings a (bored?) later reader to life. Meanwhile, we also find some traces of a scribe testing their quill, writing out the first letters of the alphabet (‘a, b, d, e, f, g’) as well as drawing a majuscule initial ’C’, perhaps trying to emulate the style of the initial ‘S’ on this page.

Here a small slip of parchment has been added to the manuscript. Slips like this were sometimes used to supply extra or missing information. If we look at a detail of the other side of the scrap (p. 52a), we can see that the pores of the animal used to make the parchment are very visible. It is possible that this parchment was deemed unfit for inclusion in a manuscript, and so was cut up to be recycled in other ways, such as for slips.

The slip has been used to provide a set of definitions from the famous encyclopedia, the Etymologiae, of Isidore of Seville. The section, taken from Etymologiae II.24.3, identifies three kinds, or ’species’, of philosophy: natural, moral, and rational. The rational type is, according to Isidore, called ‘loica’, logic, by the Greeks. Isidore defines logic as occupied with the investigation of the truth, whether regarding disputations over the causes of things or in moral matters. This slip places the study of logic within the greater context of philosophy. Along with the study of physics (nature) and ethics (morality), the study of logic is a key component of philosophy.

The ‘Porphyrian tree’

The slip (p. 52b) is found adjacent to this page, where Boethius discusses Porphyry’s definition of a species by genus and difference. Diagrams and schemes It’s possible that the scribe was prompted to add the slip by Isidore’s reference to the ‘species’ of philosophy, that is, how the genus of philosophy could be further broken down into constituent components. This ‘tree’, which is one of the earliest visualisations of Porphyry’s ideas in this form, concludes with the species ‘man’, ‘homo’. Here the classical philosophers Socrates, Cicero, and Plato, are listed as examples of the species ‘man’.

Visual representations of logic:

This part of Boethius’ commentary (Book V, 12) deals with Porphyry’s discussion of the number of feasible ways in which the five predicables – genus, species, property, difference, accident – could be combined. Here each predicable is assigned a letter, a-e. As the illustration shows, a can be combined with b, c, d, and e; b can be combined with a, c, d, e etc. However, pairings repeat: it is possible to make four individual pairings between a and the other letters. But b can only be combined into three new individual pairings, c into two new individual pairings, and so on. Ultimately, therefore, 10 individual pairings can be made between the five predicables (4+3+2+1= 10).

Einsiedeln Stiftsbibliothek Cod. 29 (878): Porphyry’s Isagoge

While Cod. 315 (605) contained Boethius’ second commentary commentary on Porphyry’s Isagoge, the Isagoge was a popular text in its own right, and often circulated indpendently, as this codex, also from Einsiedeln, illustrates. This copy of the Isagoge includes an extract from Isidore of Seville’s Etymologiae (II.25) at the beginning. The extract summarises the principal points of the Isagoge and functions as an introduction to the text.

Annotations cluster in the margins of p. 166 of Cod. 29 (878). The three annotations bracketed here (a, b, c) are all paraphrases of Boethius’ first commentary on Porphyry’s Isagoge. These annotations exemplify how medieval scholars digested commentary texts, regurgitating them into short and manageable passages of texts alongside their sources.

This manuscript also contains a Porphyrian tree (p.171). The presence of maniculae at its top and bottom, added by the fourteenth-century librarian of Einsiedeln, Heinrich von Ligerz, demonstrates the continued relevance of this logical device.

Porphyry’s Isagoge

Porphyry was a philosopher from Tyre, Phoenicia (today Lebanon), active in the third century C.E. He wrote on all the relevant branches of knowledge of the time, including philosophy, religion, grammar, and natural science, but only a portion of his work survived.

 

He became an important exponent of Neoplatonism. the dominant philosophical current of the period that reinterpreted the ideas of the ancient Greek philosopher Plato. In his philosophical writings Porphyry attempted to conciliate Aristotle’s logical works with the Platonic doctrine. 

Porphyry is best known for the Isagoge, an introduction to Aristotle's logical works that soon became an integral part of the Organon (the set of logical works of Aristotle) and one of the central texts for the study of logic in the medieval curriculum.

 

The Isagoge is an elucidation to Aristotle’s doctrine on the five universals or predicables: genus, species, difference, property, and accident. Each of these explain what a substance consists of. Taking a human for example, genus is “animal”, species “man”, difference “rational”, property “able to speak”, accident “white”. Porphyry’s Isagoge, in the Latin translation by Boethius, became widely influential in the Latin West and one of the basic introductory texts for the study of philosophy for at least 1000 years.
