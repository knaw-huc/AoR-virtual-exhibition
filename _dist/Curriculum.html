The Curriculum

What was taught in medieval schools?

In the Carolingian period, known for its efforts to revive and stimulate education, a teaching programme was promoted which included first and foremost christian learning: knowledge of the bible and the liturgy and the works of the church fathers. With that, however, a whole heritage of prechristian and late-antique learning was also adopted into the curriculum: the Roman poets for their beautiful and complex Latin, the Greek philosophers (in Latin translation) for their systems of understanding the world.

Seven Liberal Arts

From ancient times on, the body of knowledge was divided up into fields (artes or disciplinae) to make the whole more manageable. For the Latin middle ages, the division into seven arts was most influential

The Trivium

The Trivium consists of the subjects grammar, logic and rhetoric. They were the foundation of education in the Middle Ages. After learning the Trivium, students continued studying the Quadrivium: geometria, arithmetica, astronomia and musica.

MS Burney 275 contains historiated initials that depict the representations of the seven liberal arts.

Rhetoric, depicted as a woman on the left, gestures to a group of students who are clustered around a tree. The branches and leaves of the tree are decorated with the divisions of the parts of rhetoric, of oratory and of types of cases

A female representation of logic holds a book on her lap while pointing to the root of the tree of logic. On the other side of the tree two students are depicted arguing – this seems to be a representation of a disputation, a common mode of learning through debate in the medieval university. Note how the fight-with-words in the initial is mirrored by the fight-with-swords between two grotesques in the border imagery.

Curricular Texts

Logica Vetus

(Translate) De Logica Vetus zijn de teksten die het curriculum voor de studie naar logica vormden voordat andere teksten van Aristoteles over logica beschikbaar kwamen. De meeste teksten van de LV waren alleen beschikbaar in de Latijnse vertaling van Boethius die de teksten voorzag van veel commentaar.

De LV bestaat uit: De Categoriae en de De Interpretatione van Aristoteles, een introductie op de Categoriae van Porphyrius genaamd de Isagoge en vanaf de 12de eeuw ook de Liber de sex principiis van een onbekende auteur. Deze vier teksten bleven tot de 16de eeuw onderdeel van het curriculum.
